\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We deploy a multi-modal CNN-Transformer + Chronos-Bolt-mini ensemble
for ISO New England (ISO-NE) day-ahead per-zone demand forecasting as a
public web demo, and use the deployment to surface a quantitative,
reproducible drift case study. The model achieves 5.24~\% MAPE on its
training-window self-evaluation (last 2 days of 2022) and 4.21~\% MAPE
when blended per-zone with a frozen Chronos-Bolt-mini foundation model
\citep{ansari2024chronos} used zero-shot. We then re-run the same
deployed pipeline against live ISO-NE inputs at two evaluation windows
one year apart (May~2025 and May~2026, both 7~days). A
pipeline-equivalence experiment on a date inside the training window
reproduces the cluster's stored prediction to within 0.13 percentage
points (with overlapping bootstrap CIs), ruling out a serving-stack bug
as the source of the gap. The deployed system is then observed to
degrade from 5.24~\% MAPE in 2022 to 25~\% in May~2026 (baseline) and
14~\% (ensemble) \emph{[final numbers from §\ref{sec:drift}]}. The
per-zone breakdown is non-uniform: Maine and metro Boston remain
near-stationary; Rhode Island and southern/western Massachusetts
collapse to 60--80~\% MAPE. We show this signature aligns with
state-level distributed solar PV capacity per capita
\citep{eia2025distpv}, consistent with the hypothesis that
behind-the-meter solar growth in MA and RI between 2022 and 2026 is the
dominant drift mechanism.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A trained forecasting model deployed against public live data drifts as
the underlying generative process changes. For the U.S. electric grid
this matters a lot in 2026: rooftop / behind-the-meter (BTM) solar
roughly tripled in Massachusetts between 2020 and 2026
\citep{eia2025distpv,ma2024smart}, and any forecaster trained on
pre-transition data is effectively learning a stale demand
distribution. We deploy a multi-modal CNN-Transformer + Chronos-Bolt
ensemble for ISO-NE day-ahead forecasting publicly, leverage the
deployment to surface a quantitative drift case study, and isolate the
mechanism via per-zone correlation with state-level distributed PV
density.

\textbf{Why ISO-NE in particular.} The eight ISO-NE load zones span a
clean policy gradient. Massachusetts (3 zones: SEMA, WCMA, NEMA-Boston)
and Rhode Island (1 zone) have aggressive BTM solar incentives
\citep{ma2024smart,ri2024reg}; Maine, New Hampshire, and Vermont have
modest programs. Per-zone error patterns are therefore expected to
track per-state policy. All inputs needed for the deployment are
no-auth public, which lets us reproduce the entire system in a free HF
Space and a daily GitHub Actions cron.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\setlength\itemsep{1pt}
    \item A public end-to-end deployment of a multimodal forecaster
    (HF Space + auxiliary GitHub-Actions data repo) that serves a
    daily-refreshed 7-day rolling backtest at no ongoing cost
    (§\ref{sec:setup}).
    \item A pipeline-equivalence experiment: re-running the deployed
    pipeline on a date inside the training window reproduces the
    cluster's stored prediction to within 0.13~pp on overall MAPE, with
    overlapping bootstrap CIs (§\ref{sec:validation}).
    \item A two-window drift quantification (W1 = 2025-05;
    W2 = 2026-04/05) with bootstrap 95~\% CIs and three naive baselines
    (persistence-1d, persistence-7d, climatological mean), showing a
    monotone year-over-year MAPE escalation that is consistent with
    state-level BTM solar growth (§\ref{sec:drift}--§\ref{sec:btm}).
\end{enumerate}

The artifacts---trained baseline, Chronos integration, deployment
infrastructure, and rolling backtest data---are all publicly accessible
(§\ref{app:repro}).
